Why and how is music so beneficial?

While listening to music, dopamine — a neurotransmitter that gives one pleasure — is released in the brain and in turn, the listener’s mood will improve. Furthermore, the body sends out music-induced chills as a way to show musical pleasure. In a neurological sense, these chills reduce anxiety in the brain. With music and its effects, patients can feel more energized and happy.

In addition to improving one’s mood, music can also help with one’s memory and learning. Memories are created through remembering a situation repeatedly in one’s mind which in turn strengthens the bond between neurons.Two types of waves are used when one remembers something. The alpha waves in the brain are responsible for one’s concentration and the theta waves are responsible for one’s calmness. However, when one can not remember a certain piece of information, it is because stress levels in the brain rise and lower theta waves are present. Stress can decrease the amount of neurons in the brain as well as how they are formed which in turn limits one’s memory. As stated before, music relaxes the brain by releasing dopamine and therefore increasing theta waves and lowering stress levels.

In a TED talk by neuroscientist and musician Alan Harvey, he explores the power of music and its impact on the human brain.
Harvey dives into the neuroscience of music, explaining how music can stimulate our emotions, memories, and even our reward center. He argues that music is not just an art form, but a fundamental human capability that has played a vital role in our evolution.



Our brain is activated by all sorts of music, regardless of the genre. Researchers used fMRI to scan the speaker's brain while he listened to music. The scans showed activity in almost all areas of the brain, not just one specific area.
Music activates different parts of the brain depending on the function. The auditory cortex tracks loudness, pitch, and timbre. The visual cortex is activated when reading music. The motor cortex is activated when we tap our feet or clap our hands in time with the music. The cerebellum mediates emotional responses, and the hippocampus is involved in memory.
